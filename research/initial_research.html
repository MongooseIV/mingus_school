Initial Research

Problem Statement:

How do English speakers' biases about language influence their phonaesthetic perceptions?
Do words' semantic connections affect perceived euphony/cacophony?
Do biases about other languages affect percieved euphony/cacophony?

Need Statement:

Phonaesthetics is an under researched field of linguistics. While some studies have been conducted, they are focused in their scope and leave a lot of questions to be investigated.

Phonaesthetics: the study of the pleasantness of speech sounds.

Euphony: sounding pleasant

Cacophony: sounding unpleasant

Some sounds have been found to be considered more euphonic than others (such as /l, n, m, s, k, r, t, d/), others more cacophonous (such as /ð, ʒ, θ/). However, this data is likely by influenced by semantic connections.

(Crystal 1995)

Romantic languages tend to be percieved as more beautiful than others. In addition, the size of a language's L2 (second langauge) community often affects variance in its aesthetic ratings more than the size of its L1 (native speaking) community.

(Kogan VV and Reiterer SM 2021)
